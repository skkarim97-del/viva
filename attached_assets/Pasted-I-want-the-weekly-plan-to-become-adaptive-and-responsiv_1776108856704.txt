I want the weekly plan to become adaptive and responsive to patient feedback from the Today screen.

Right now, the weekly plan should not be static.
It should update based on how the patient is feeling today and recently, so the app can prevent symptoms or poor recovery from snowballing through the week.

Important context:
The app can absolutely use internal severity logic to drive these changes, but patients should NOT see explicit severity states, color-coded risk levels, or anything alarming.

Do NOT expose:
- green / yellow / orange / red
- risk levels
- alert-style severity labels
- “your plan was downgraded”
- anything that could make the patient panic

Instead:
- use severity states internally only
- translate them into calm, supportive, treatment-aware language
- make the plan feel smart, responsive, and reassuring

=====================================
GOAL
=====================================

Refactor the weekly plan system so that:

1. it generates a baseline weekly plan
2. it applies adaptive overrides based on recent patient inputs
3. it becomes more defensive when the patient is struggling
4. it becomes more progressive when the patient is doing well

The weekly plan should feel alive, intelligent, and treatment-aware.

=====================================
PART 1: BASELINE + ADAPTIVE MODEL
=====================================

Structure weekly plan logic into 2 layers:

1. Baseline Plan
Generated from:
- onboarding/profile
- medication + dose
- dose frequency
- recent titration
- Apple Health trends
- historical patterns

2. Adaptive Override Layer
Generated from:
- today’s inputs
- last 2–3 days of inputs
- symptom severity
- energy
- appetite
- digestion / nausea
- mental state
- treatment logging

Final weekly plan should be:
baseline plan + adaptive adjustments

=====================================
PART 2: INTERNAL SEVERITY STATES ONLY
=====================================

Create a simple internal severity framework that can influence the weekly plan.

These states are INTERNAL ONLY and should NOT be shown directly to the patient.

Examples:

GREEN
- good energy
- normal appetite
- mild or no symptoms

YELLOW
- tired or slightly low
- appetite lower than normal
- mild symptoms

ORANGE
- low energy
- low appetite
- moderate nausea / digestion issues
- mentally low

RED
- depleted
- very low appetite
- severe nausea
- diarrhea
- burnt out mentally

This internal state should influence not just today’s plan, but the next several days of the weekly plan.

Again:
Do NOT show these labels or colors in patient-facing UI.

=====================================
PART 3: WEEKLY PLAN ADJUSTMENT RULES
=====================================

Please create rules like:

RED STATE (internal only):
Example inputs:
- depleted energy
- very low appetite
- severe nausea
- diarrhea
- burnt out mentally

Then:
- today = full recovery mode
- next 2–3 days = simplified support plan
- reduce movement intensity sharply
- prioritize hydration + electrolytes
- simplify meals / protein support
- increase rest / sleep emphasis
- reduce training progression this week
- allow weekly plan to become more defensive overall

ORANGE STATE (internal only):
Example:
- tired
- low appetite
- moderate nausea or digestion disruption
- mentally low

Then:
- today = lighter support day
- next 1–2 days = recovery-biased plan
- shift movement toward walking / light activity
- increase hydration / fuel emphasis
- avoid overloading the rest of the week

YELLOW STATE (internal only):
Example:
- slightly tired
- appetite somewhat low
- mild symptoms

Then:
- soften plan slightly
- make next 1–2 days a little lighter
- keep routines simple
- do not fully pull back the week unless pattern persists

GREEN STATE (internal only):
Example:
- good energy
- normal appetite
- mild/no symptoms

Then:
- allow plan to proceed
- moderate / progressive movement is okay
- maintain normal hydration, recovery, and fueling structure

=====================================
PART 4: PREVENT SNOWBALLING
=====================================

This is very important.

The purpose of this logic is not just to react today.
It is to prevent the patient from digging a deeper hole for the rest of the week.

So if severe or moderate symptoms are logged, the weekly plan should actively reduce the chance of further snowballing by:
- lowering intensity
- simplifying recovery demands
- prioritizing treatment tolerance
- preventing overexertion

=====================================
PART 5: DURATION OF PLAN ADJUSTMENTS
=====================================

Adjustments should not always last the entire week.

Use logic like:

- severe state -> 2–3 day defensive adjustment
- moderate state -> 1–2 day lighter adjustment
- mild state -> small same-day or next-day modification
- stable / improving state -> return toward baseline plan

The weekly plan should be able to recover back toward baseline if the patient starts doing better.

=====================================
PART 6: PATIENT-FACING LANGUAGE
=====================================

This is critical.

The patient should NOT see:
- red / yellow / orange / green
- high / medium / low risk
- severe / moderate / mild state labels
- anything alarming or clinical

Instead, translate the internal logic into calm, supportive language.

Examples of patient-facing language:

Instead of showing RED:
- “Let’s keep things simple for the next couple days”
- “A lighter couple of days will likely help”
- “This week may go better with more recovery and simpler routines”

Instead of showing ORANGE:
- “A bit more recovery will help here”
- “Let’s ease up slightly for the next day or two”
- “A lighter stretch will likely work better right now”

Instead of showing YELLOW:
- “Keep things steady today”
- “Simple routines will go a long way here”
- “A small adjustment should help”

Instead of showing GREEN:
- “You’re in a good spot to keep building”
- “This looks like a good time to stay consistent”
- “You can keep the week moving as planned”

Tone should always be:
- supportive
- calm
- non-alarming
- non-judgmental
- treatment-aware
- human

Do NOT make this look alarming.

Just make it feel responsive and smart.
