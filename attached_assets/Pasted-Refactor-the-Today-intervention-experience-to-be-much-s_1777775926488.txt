Refactor the Today intervention experience to be much simpler, friendlier and easier for an average GLP-1 patient to understand.

The current version is too heavy. It has too many boxes, labels, chips and summary stats. It feels more like a system dashboard than a patient support experience.

Goal:
Create a much simpler, more intuitive intervention UX that still preserves the core value prop:
- Viva notices issues between visits
- Viva gives the patient a clear next step
- Viva captures feedback
- Viva escalates only if needed
- the experience feels calm, supportive and easy to engage with

Design principles:
- prioritize clarity over complexity
- one clear next step first
- progressive disclosure for secondary actions
- warm, friendly language
- minimal visual clutter
- keep it lightweight for first-time users

Implement the following changes:

1. Simplify the card header
Use:

Title:
“Today’s next steps”

Subtitle:
“Based on your check-in, here’s what may help today.”

Keep the overall light blue Viva card style.

2. Simplify the “What Viva noticed” section
Replace the current longer explanation with a short, patient-friendly summary.

Example:
“What we noticed”
“You reported nausea, low appetite and low energy today.”

Do not add extra explanation about issue counts or prioritization here.

3. Remove the summary stats module
Remove:
- “4 issues surfaced”
- “1 needs attention now”
- “3 supportive steps”

These are too clinical and create clutter.

4. Remove the progress bar and progress count from the top
Do not show “0 of 4 steps started” or a progress bar at the top of the experience.

If progress is kept at all, it should be very subtle and secondary, not a dominant element.

5. Replace the large multi-card list with one primary action first
Show one primary intervention card at the top.

Use the highest-priority symptom category to choose the primary recommendation.
For current demo data, nausea should be primary.

Structure:

Small badge:
“Start with this”

Title:
Simple, action-oriented title, not technical.
Example:
“Eat something light and sip water slowly”

Body:
“Try a small bland snack like yogurt, soup or a smoothie, and take a few slow sips of water.”

Helper line:
“This may help with nausea and make eating feel easier.”

Buttons:
Primary: “I’ll try this”
Secondary: “Another idea”

Important:
Use plain, human-friendly language.
Avoid overly clinical labels like “Nausea support” as the main headline if a simpler action phrase works better.

6. Secondary actions should be visually lighter
Below the primary action, add a section:
“Other things that may help today”

This section should be compact and visually secondary.

Show the remaining recommendations as smaller rows, not full large cards.

Each row should have:
- short title
- one-line description
- small action button or tap target

Example rows:
- “Add a protein-rich snack later”
- “Take a short break to rest”
- “Add fiber and keep sipping water”

Do not show giant stacked full cards for all recommendations by default.

7. Keep per-row state logic, but simplify the presentation
Preserve the existing interaction model:
- default
- committed
- better / no_change / worse
- escalation only after worse

But present it more simply.

After a patient taps “I’ll try this”, show:
“How do you feel after trying it?”

Buttons:
- “Better”
- “About the same”
- “Worse”

Use “About the same” instead of “No change” because it is more natural.

8. Simplify “Not for me”
Replace “Not for me” with “Another idea” on the primary recommendation.

For secondary rows, you may keep:
- “Try this”
or
- tap row to open

If the user taps “Another idea”, show an alternate suggestion for the same issue.

Example for nausea:
“Try crackers, ginger tea or another bland snack, and avoid a large meal for now.”

Then allow:
- “I’ll try this”
- “Back”

9. Escalation only when needed
If the patient taps “Worse”, show:
“Sorry that didn’t help. Would you like another suggestion or support from your care team?”

Buttons:
- “Try another idea”
- “Ask my care team”

Do not show “Ask my care team” by default anywhere else.

10. Tone and copy
Make the tone:
- supportive
- simple
- calm
- clear

Avoid:
- overly clinical phrasing
- jargon
- dense prioritization language
- too much text

11. Visual simplification
Make the UI cleaner by:
- removing most chips and status labels
- reducing repeated card styling
- reducing vertical clutter
- using one prominent primary card and compact secondary rows
- preserving the Viva palette and rounded styling

12. Keep analytics and backend behavior intact
Do not break current behavior:
- onAccept
- onFeedback
- escalation callback
- AsyncStorage / hydration / persistence logic

Only simplify and improve the presentation and patient-facing interaction flow.

13. Verification
Run:
- typecheck
- lint if available
- mobile/web preview

Acceptance criteria:
- a first-time GLP-1 patient can immediately understand what Viva is asking them to do
- the primary action is obvious
- the experience feels simple and supportive
- secondary recommendations are available but visually lighter
- escalation appears only when needed
- the screen feels like a helpful guide, not a dashboard
