Upgrade the Personalized Check-in intervention experience into an enterprise-grade guided intervention flow.

Current issue:
The app now shows multiple recommendation rows, but it still feels like a static checklist. For an enterprise-grade clinical product, the UX should feel like an intelligent intervention layer that prioritizes patient issues, guides the patient through the next best action, captures outcomes and escalates only when automation is insufficient.

Goal:
Build a 10/10 UX for patient intervention:
- clear prioritization
- urgency without alarm
- automated first-line support
- patient-friendly rationale
- measurable action tracking
- outcome feedback
- escalation only when clinically or behaviorally appropriate
- polished enterprise-level interaction design

Do not revert the current per-row interaction model.

Keep:
- all relevant recommendations visible immediately
- per-row interaction state
- “I’ll try this” / “Not for me”
- outcome feedback: Better / No change / Worse
- escalation only after Worse or repeated unresolved issues
- no default “Ask my care team” button

Implement the following:

1. Reframe the section from “recommendations” to “support plan”
Rename or visually frame the recommendation area as:

“Today’s support plan”

Under it, add a concise explanation:
“Viva prioritized the steps most likely to help based on today’s check-in.”

This should make the product feel intelligent, not generic.

2. Add an issue severity / priority summary
Above the recommendations, add a compact summary module.

Example:
“4 issues surfaced”
“1 needs attention now”
“3 supportive steps”

For the current demo state:
- nausea = priority issue
- low appetite, low energy and constipation = supportive issues

Use calm clinical language. Avoid scary copy.

3. Add a primary intervention card
The highest-priority issue should render as a distinct primary card.

Priority ranking:
1. Severe nausea
2. Moderate nausea
3. Very low appetite
4. Low appetite
5. Very low energy
6. Low energy
7. Constipation
8. Hydration

For the current demo, nausea should be the primary intervention.

Primary card structure:

Badge:
“Start here”

Title:
“Nausea support”

Subtitle:
“Most important next step”

Body:
“Try a small, bland protein-forward snack like yogurt, soup or a smoothie, and sip water slowly.”

Rationale line:
“Why this matters: nausea and low intake can make it harder to stay on track today.”

Time cue:
“Try within the next hour”

Buttons:
Primary: “I’ll try this”
Secondary: “Not for me”

4. Add secondary intervention rows
All remaining recommendations should render below as secondary rows/cards.

Structure:
Title
Timing badge
One concise body sentence
Button row

Examples:

Appetite support
Timing badge: “Next meal”
Body: “Add one protein-rich snack today, like Greek yogurt, tofu or a smoothie.”

Energy support
Timing badge: “Helpful today”
Body: “Add protein to your next meal and take a short break to rest.”

Constipation support
Timing badge: “This afternoon”
Body: “Add a fiber-rich food to your next meal and sip water steadily through the afternoon.”

Buttons:
Primary: “I’ll try this”
Secondary: “Not for me”

Secondary rows should be more compact than the primary card.

5. Add live progress tracking
Add a small progress module above the intervention rows.

Example:
“0 of 4 steps started”

As users tap “I’ll try this”, update the count:
“1 of 4 steps started”

Add a very lightweight progress bar or segmented indicator if simple to implement.

Do not overbuild this. It should feel motivating but not gamified.

6. Add smart status chips on each row
Each row should show a status chip based on state:

Default:
“Ready”

Committed:
“Started”

Not for me:
“Skipped”

Better:
“Helped”

No change:
“Still tracking”

Worse:
“Needs follow-up”

Use subtle colors consistent with the existing design.

7. Improve committed state
When a user taps “I’ll try this”, transform that row into a follow-up state.

Copy:
“Once you’ve tried it, how do you feel?”

Buttons:
- “Better”
- “No change”
- “Worse”

Also show:
“Viva will use this to personalize your next step.”

8. Improve “Not for me” state
When user taps “Not for me”, do not make the row dead.

Show:
“Got it. Want a different option?”

Buttons:
- “Show another option”
- “Skip for today”

For now, if alternate recommendations are not available, “Show another option” can cycle to a fallback suggestion for the same category.

Fallback suggestions:

Nausea:
“Try ginger tea, crackers or another bland snack, and avoid large portions for now.”

Appetite:
“Try a few bites of something easy to tolerate, like yogurt, tofu, soup or a smoothie.”

Energy:
“Take a short rest, hydrate slowly and add protein when you can.”

Constipation:
“Try a fiber-rich food like berries, chia, beans or vegetables, and keep sipping water.”

Hydration:
“Take a few small sips every 5 to 10 minutes for the next hour.”

9. Improve “No change” state
When user taps “No change”, show a supportive automated next step.

Copy:

Buttons:
- “Try another step”
- “Keep tracking”

Do not escalate immediately on “No change” unless this issue has repeated across multiple check-ins.

10. Improve “Worse” state
When user taps “Worse”, show a stronger but calm escalation panel.

Copy:

Buttons:
- “Try another step”
- “Ask my care team”

Only fire the care-team escalation callback when “Ask my care team” is tapped.

- “No change” across multiple check-ins, or
- “Not for me” repeatedly
