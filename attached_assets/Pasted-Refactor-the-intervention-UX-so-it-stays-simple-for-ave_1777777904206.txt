Refactor the intervention UX so it stays simple for average GLP-1 patients, but the recommendations themselves are stronger clinical micro-protocols that can actually help mitigate symptoms and support treatment persistence.

Important context:
This feature is a major part of Viva’s value prop. It should not feel like generic wellness advice such as “eat a little” or “sip water.” It needs to feel like automated first-line symptom support that can reduce side-effect burden, keep patients on treatment and save providers time.

Goal:
Keep the simplified guided UX:
- one clear primary next step visible first
- secondary ideas collapsed or lighter
- outcome feedback after action
- escalation only if symptoms worsen or unresolved issues repeat

But upgrade the intervention content:
- more specific
- more clinically useful
- still patient-friendly
- not overly technical
- clear enough for a normal patient to follow

1. Keep the guided single-step structure

Default view should still show:
- Today’s next step
- What we noticed
- one primary intervention
- collapsed “More support for today”
- no default Ask my care team button

Do not return to showing 4 large cards by default.

2. Update primary card copy to be more clinically meaningful

For nausea primary recommendation, use:

Badge:
“Start here”

Title:
“Settle nausea without skipping nutrition”

Body:
“Try 3 to 5 bites of a bland protein option like Greek yogurt, tofu, soup or a smoothie. Then take small sips of water over 20 to 30 minutes. Avoid greasy, spicy or large meals for now.”

Helper line:
“This helps reduce nausea while keeping some protein and fluids in your system.”

Buttons:
- “I’ll try this”
- “Show me another option”

3. Use stronger symptom-specific micro-protocols

Update recommendation builder copy to use more concrete first-line support.

Nausea support:
Title:
“Settle nausea without skipping nutrition”
Body:
“Try 3 to 5 bites of a bland protein option like Greek yogurt, tofu, soup or a smoothie. Then take small sips of water over 20 to 30 minutes. Avoid greasy, spicy or large meals for now.”
Helper:
“This helps reduce nausea while keeping some protein and fluids in your system.”

Nausea alternate:
Title:
“Try something even gentler”
Body:
“Try crackers, ginger tea or a few bites of soup. Keep portions small and pause if nausea increases.”
Helper:
“Small amounts are often easier to tolerate than a full meal.”

Low appetite support:
Title:
“Protect your protein intake”
Body:
“Aim for a small protein serving every few hours today, even if it is only Greek yogurt, tofu, soup or a smoothie.”
Helper:
“This can help prevent low intake from turning into low energy or missed nutrition.”

Low appetite alternate:
Title:
“Use liquid nutrition if solid food feels hard”
Body:
“Try a smoothie, protein shake or soup instead of a full meal. Take it slowly over 20 to 30 minutes.”
Helper:
“Liquid options can be easier when appetite is low.”

Low energy support:
Title:
“Support energy without forcing a meal”
Body:
“Try a small protein plus carb option like yogurt with fruit, soup with tofu or a smoothie, then rest for 10 minutes.”
Helper:
“This gives your body fuel without requiring a large meal.”

Low energy alternate:
Title:
“Reset with fluids and a short break”
Body:
“Take small sips of water, sit or lie down for 10 minutes, then try a small snack if you feel ready.”
Helper:
“Low intake and dehydration can make fatigue worse.”

Constipation support:
Title:
“Reduce constipation risk today”
Body:
“Add fiber gradually with foods like berries, chia, beans or vegetables. Keep sipping fluids and take a short walk if you can tolerate it.”
Helper:
“Fiber works best when paired with fluids and movement.”

Constipation alternate:
Title:
“Use a gentler bowel-support step”
Body:
“Try warm fluids, a short walk or a fiber-rich snack. Avoid suddenly adding a large amount of fiber at once.”
Helper:
“Gradual changes are less likely to worsen bloating.”

Hydration support:
Title:
“Rehydrate without upsetting your stomach”
Body:
“Take a few small sips every 5 to 10 minutes for the next hour. If plain water feels hard, try an electrolyte drink or diluted beverage.”
Helper:
“Small, steady sips are usually easier than drinking a lot at once.”

Hydration alternate:
Title:
“Try fluids that are easier to tolerate”
Body:
“Try ice chips, warm tea, diluted juice or an electrolyte drink. Keep the amount small and steady.”
Helper:
“The goal is steady fluids without triggering nausea.”

4. Prioritize recommendations by symptom impact

Select the primary intervention using this order:
1. moderate or severe nausea
2. very low appetite
3. constipation
4. low appetite
5. low energy
6. low hydration

Reason:
Nausea and low intake can drive discontinuation because they make patients feel like treatment is intolerable. Constipation can also become a major persistence issue if not managed early.

5. Improve “What we noticed” with simple clinical logic

Example:
“You reported nausea, low appetite, low energy and constipation today. We’ll start with nausea because it can make eating and hydration harder.”

Keep this short.

6. Rename collapsed secondary section

Use:
“More support for today”

Subtitle:
“Other steps that may help with appetite, energy or digestion.”

Collapsed by default.

Expanded rows should be compact and show:
- action-oriented title
- one-line body

Buttons:

8. Guardrail copy

Keep a subtle footer:
“Viva supports between-visit care. If symptoms feel severe, urgent or unusual, contact your care team or seek medical help.”

9. Do not make medical claims too strongly

Avoid wording like:
- “This will fix nausea”
- “This prevents stopping treatment”
- “This treats side effects”

Use:
- “may help”
- “can support”
- “helps reduce risk”
- “can make symptoms easier to manage”
