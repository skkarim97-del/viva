Refine the new patient summary layer on the patient detail page so it is conceptually cleaner and less fragmented. The main issue to fix is that the current summary logic treats “no check-in / silence” too similarly to actual clinical deterioration. We need to clearly separate engagement risk from clinical risk, while keeping the page simple and preserving the current MVP structure.
Core goal
When a clinician opens a patient page, the summary at the top should make it immediately obvious whether the issue is:


Engagement


Clinical


Combined


Stable


Right now, “no check-in in 10 days” is being surfaced in a way that can feel too close to a true clinical problem. That is not ideal. Silence matters, but it should usually mean disengagement / retention risk, not automatically clinical worsening.
What to change
1. Add an “Issue type” label to the top summary card
Inside the existing Patient summary card, add a small pill or label that classifies the patient into one of these types:


Engagement


Clinical


Combined


Stable


This should be visually subtle but clear.
2. Update summary logic so engagement and clinical concerns are distinct
Revise the summary generation so it follows this framework:
Engagement
Use this when the main issue is things like:


no recent check-in


missed engagement


follow-up pending without clear symptom issue


patient requested review but no clinical deterioration signal


low responsiveness / possible disengagement


Example summaries:


“Patient has not checked in for 10 days and may be disengaging.”


“Patient requested follow-up and has not yet re-engaged.”


“Engagement has slowed and follow-up may be needed.”


Clinical
Use this when the main issue is symptom or treatment related:


worsening nausea


worsening fatigue / energy


treatment tolerance issue


adherence concern


stopped treatment


risk drivers that reflect actual patient condition


Example summaries:


“Patient is reporting worsening nausea and may need treatment support.”


“Symptoms appear to be worsening and clinician review may be appropriate.”


“Treatment tolerance may be declining based on recent check-ins.”


Combined
Use this when both are true:


symptom issue followed by silence


elevated symptom burden plus missed check-ins


treatment concern plus unresolved engagement drop


Example summaries:


“Patient reported worsening symptoms and then stopped checking in.”


“Recent treatment concerns are now paired with declining engagement.”


“Patient may be struggling with both treatment tolerance and follow-through.”


Stable
Use this when nothing meaningful is wrong:


no strong symptom signal


no important engagement issue


no unresolved escalation


no pending follow-up


Example:


“Patient appears stable with no immediate follow-up needed.”


3. Keep action priority, but do not let silence alone feel clinically severe
Retain the Action priority pill:


Review now


Follow up today


Monitor


Stable


But make sure the logic treats silence differently from symptom worsening.
Guidance:


No check-in / silence should usually drive Follow up today or Monitor


Worsening symptoms or explicit treatment concern can drive stronger urgency


Combined engagement + symptom concern can justify stronger escalation


Do not make silence alone sound like clinical deterioration


4. Update reason pills so they are grouped more intelligently
Keep the “Why this patient is surfaced” row, but revise the logic so the reasons naturally reflect the issue type.
Examples:
Engagement reasons


No check-in 10 days


Follow-up pending


Patient requested review


Low engagement


Clinical reasons


Nausea worsened


Energy declined


Side effects elevated


Treatment stopped


Last check-in negative


Combined reasons
Mix both types when both are present.
Keep the max at 2 to 4 pills and prioritize the most useful ones.
5. Reduce duplication between the summary card and the separate Last check-in alert
Right now the page feels a bit fragmented because the Patient summary card already says something like “Patient has not checked in for 10 days,” and then the pink Last check-in alert below repeats the same thing.
Please clean this up.
Best option:


keep the Last check-in alert only when it adds important supporting context


or restyle / tone it down so it feels secondary


or integrate the “last check-in” fact into the summary layer more gracefully


Do not remove useful recency information, but reduce repetitive visual duplication.
6. Preserve the rest of the page
Do not redesign the full page.
Do not remove:


Treatment status


Why this risk band


Recent check-ins


Notes


existing follow-up / escalation CTAs


The current structure is mostly good. This is a refinement, not a rebuild.
7. Suggested logic framework
Use current available client-side data only. No heavy backend changes needed.
Think of the classification like this:


If there are both symptom / treatment concerns and engagement issues → Combined


Else if the strongest issue is symptom / treatment related → Clinical


Else if the strongest issue is non-response / missed check-in / unresolved follow-up → Engagement


Else → Stable


Signals that count as engagement:


silentDays


followUpPending


escalationOpen without symptom issue


no recent check-ins


missed engagement pattern


Signals that count as clinical:


symptom flags


risk drivers tied to condition


treatment stopped


severe symptom tags


negative recent symptom pattern


8. Product intent
The top of the page should now read like:


what type of problem this is


what is happening


what to do next


Not just:


generic risk


generic follow-up


That distinction will make the dashboard feel much smarter and more clinically credible.
Final result we want
For a patient who has not checked in for 10 days but has no strong symptom issue:


Issue type: Engagement


Summary: “Patient has not checked in for 10 days and may be disengaging.”


Priority: Follow up today


Next action: “Reach out to re-engage this patient.”


For a patient with worsening nausea but still engaging:


Issue type: Clinical


Summary: “Patient is reporting worsening nausea and may need treatment support.”


Priority based on severity


Next action focused on symptom review


For a patient with worsening symptoms and then silence:


Issue type: Combined


Summary: “Patient reported worsening symptoms and then stopped checking in.”


Priority stronger than either issue alone


The result should feel more unified, more intelligent, and less like separate components repeating themselves.
